# Tuned Yota — NEPQ Sales & Messaging Playbook
*Source: "The NEPQ Black Book of Questions" (7th Level / Jeremy Miner). Adapted for Tuned Yota's lead and client conversations across email, Facebook Messenger, Instagram DM, and other channels.*

---

## 1. The Core Philosophy (apply to EVERY message we send)

**We are problem finders and problem solvers — not product pushers.** The top 1% of salespeople don't pitch; they ask skilled questions that get the prospect to tell THEMSELVES why they need to change. Internal motivation (their own reasons) never wears off. External pressure (our pitch) does.

**The doctor frame.** A good doctor never prescribes before diagnosing. Tuned Yota never quotes, pitches, or "sends info" before we understand the customer's truck, their frustrations with it, and what they're actually trying to achieve. When we diagnose first, we become the **trusted authority** — and prospects start chasing us instead of us chasing them.

**Turn statements into questions.** Instead of telling a lead what a tune will do, ask questions that get them to describe what their truck is doing (or not doing) and how that affects them. If WE state the problem and solution, we own it and they detach. If THEY say it, they own it — and that's what sells.

**Pain drives the sale.** Without uncovered pain, there is no urgency and no sale. Our questions must surface the gap between what their truck does now and what they want it to do — and let them feel that gap. The more clearly a prospect experiences their pain, the more the price of solving it feels small.

**Answer a question, then immediately ask one back.** Never let a reply end with only information. Every message we send should end with exactly ONE question (text channels) that moves the conversation one stage forward. Whoever is asking questions is guiding the conversation.

**Neutral, low-pressure language always.** Words like "might," "possibly," "would you be open to...," "just to see if I could even help you" lower sales resistance and raise status. Never use pressure phrases like "make an informed decision," "act now," "best on the market" — prospects have defense mechanisms wired for those.

## 2. The NEPQ Conversation Sequence

Every lead conversation, regardless of channel, follows this arc. In DMs it may span 10–20 short messages over hours or days; in email, 2–4 exchanges. Never skip stages, never jump to price.

**Stage 1 — Connect (put the focus on them, not us)**
- "Have you found what you're looking for, or are you still looking?"
- "Curious — what was it about the [tune/build/post] that caught your attention?"
- "Anything else that got your attention?"

**Stage 2 — Situation (their present setup; 3–4 questions MAX)**
- "What are you running now — year/model, stock or already modded?"
- "How long have you had it? What do you mainly use it for — daily, towing, off-road, all of it?"
- "Have you done any tuning or bolt-ons before, or would this be your first?"

**Stage 3 — Problem Awareness (do they like their current situation? 3–4+ questions)**
- "What do you like about how it drives now? What don't you like?"
- "Why does that bother you though?" (dig on whatever they name)
- "Is there anything else you'd change about how it runs if you could?"
- Two Truths (when things "seem fine"): "Sounds like the truck's treating you fairly well — is there anything you'd change about it if you could?" → "Why would you change that?" → "Why is that important to you now though?"

**Stage 4 — Solution Awareness (their ideas; they own the problem emotionally, 2–3 questions)**
- "Have you looked around at anything that would get you what you're wanting?"
- "What have you tried so far, if anything? How did that work out?"
- "What's stopped you from getting it handled before now?"
- "If it were dialed in exactly the way you want — how would that change things for you?"

**Stage 5 — Consequence (cost of doing nothing; 1–2 questions, use with care)**
- "What happens if you don't do anything about it and it keeps [hesitating on the highway / drinking fuel / falling on its face towing]?"
- "Have you thought about what it'll be like keeping it this way for the next 5 years?"

**Stage 6 — Qualifying (importance + urgency; 1–2 questions)**
- "How important is it for you to fix this and get the truck [what they said they want]?"
- "Why now though?" (the single most powerful urgency question — always ask it)

**Stage 7 — Transition (the ONLY place we "present," and only to their stated problems)**
- "Based on what you've told me, what we do might actually be a fit — because you said [their logical problem], and that's making you [their emotional frustration]. What we'd do is [1–2 sentences tying OUR solution directly to THEIR stated problems — nothing more]. Does that feel like it might be what you're looking for?"
- Follow with: "Why do you feel like it is though?" (they close themselves)

**Stage 8 — Commit (calm, assumptive, low-friction)**
- "Do you feel like this could be the answer for you? Why though?"
- "Really the next step is we get you on the schedule / get your deposit in, and from there we [next steps]. Would that be appropriate, or how would you like to proceed?"

## 3. Channel Rules

**Instagram / Facebook DMs**
- ONE question per message. Short, casual, lowercase-friendly. Match their energy and vocabulary.
- Mirror their words back ("you said it falls on its face towing — how long has that been going on?"). Their own words are the most persuasive words available.
- Voice notes (where appropriate) build trust faster mid-conversation — tone carries curiosity better than text.
- Never send a wall of specs, a price list, or a link as the first response to an inquiry. Diagnose first.
- If they go quiet, follow up by re-opening THEIR pain, not our offer: "hey [name] — were you able to get the hesitation sorted, or is it still doing it?"

**Email**
- 2–3 questions max per email, framed conversationally. Short paragraphs.
- Subject lines reference THEIR situation, not our product ("your 3rd gen towing question" beats "Tuned Yota Packages & Pricing").
- Every email ends with one clear question or one clear micro-commitment.
- Quotes/proposals follow the Proposal Rules in §5 — never as a bare price in a first reply.

**Comments → DM**
- Reply publicly with a connecting question, take it to DMs: "what year are you running? shoot me a DM and I'll take a look at what's possible for yours."

**Phone/Video (when a DM/email lead escalates)**
- Open like the trusted authority: "Just so I don't go over things you already know — what would be the most valuable way for us to spend this time? What are you hoping I can help with?"

## 4. Scripts for the Money Moments

### "How much for a tune?" (the #1 inbound message)
Never answer with a number first. Never dodge rudely either. Deflect-with-purpose:
> "Happy to get you an exact number — it depends on what you're running and what you want out of it. What year/model, and what's it doing now that you want changed?"

Then run the sequence. Price comes AFTER problems and goals are on the table, framed against their stated pain.

### "Just send me pricing / info."
Black Book Rule: NEVER send info without first finding out if this is a serious person wanting to change their situation.
> "That's not a problem — just so I put together the right info for you, what exactly are you looking for it to do?"
> (after they answer) "Ok, and what's it doing now for [that]?" → you're now in the engagement stage naturally.

Before finally sending anything, lock the next step:
> "I'll get that over to you. Let's suppose you look it over and it fits what you're looking for — what would you want the next step to be?"
If they can't answer that, they're fishing or unqualified — deprioritize.

### "Will it void my warranty?" / technical objections
Don't argue or lecture. Get behind the concern with questions:
> "Fair question — what's your biggest concern there exactly?" → understand it → help them resolve it themselves: "If that part were covered to your satisfaction, is this something that would work for you? Why do you feel it would?"

### "I need to think about it."
> "That's fine — usually when someone says that, there's something specific they're not 100% on. What part are you still unsure about?"
or: "I sense some hesitation — can you help me understand what's behind that?"

### "Let me check with my wife/husband/partner."
> "Makes sense. Just to see — if it were only up to you, is this something you'd move on? Why though?" → "What do you think they'll want to know? I can put together exactly that so the conversation's easy."

### "I'll get back to you." (the disappearing lead)
Position as the busy authority, don't chase:
> "No problem. What's your timeframe on getting back to me, just so I can see if I'd still have a slot open for you?"
> Then: "If you've got your calendar handy I'll pull up mine and we'll set a time so you're not having to chase me down and vice versa — would that be appropriate?"

### "Can you send me some reviews/references?"
Never just send them — that's often a brush-off. Qualify it:
> "Not a problem — curious, what would you want to ask them / see from them?" → "Let's pretend they check out and it's all good — where do you think we go from there?"
No commitment on the other side of the references = don't invest the effort.

### "I don't have the money right now."
> "Tell me — if the funds weren't an issue, is this something that would work for you? Why do you feel it would?" → "And I get that money might be tight from what you said — how do you think you could resolve that so you can [repeat what they said they want]?" → if stuck: "Can I make a suggestion? What some of our customers do when they're short is [payment plan / staged build / save the date with a deposit]. Which of those could work for you?"

### Price shoppers ("Shop X does it for $200 less")
Shift from lowest-price thinking to results thinking:
> "Can I ask — you mentioned price is the big thing. How does that compare to actually getting the result you told me you want and not having to do this twice?"
> "Let's suppose a few shops all hit your budget. How would you decide between them then?"

## 5. Quote / Proposal Rules (from "The Proposal Process")

1. **Never quote before discovery.** No exceptions — even when they ask for it upfront. Use: "I'd be open to putting a quote together, but I'm not sure I could even help you yet — could I ask a few questions about your setup first so what I send is actually useful?"
2. **Every quote restates THEIR 2–3 stated problems and THEIR 2–3 goals** — in their words — before any pricing. This proves we listened and re-triggers the pain the price solves.
3. **Always present 3 options:** basic (entry, still profitable) / core (where most land) / premium. The premium anchor makes the core option feel like a bargain and lifts average order value.
4. **Frame value at ~10x price wherever honest** (fuel savings over ownership, avoiding parts-cannon waste, resale, not doing it twice, time saved).
5. **Make the quote actionable** — deposit link or "reply YES and I'll slot you in." Every extra step between yes and paid is a place the deal dies.
6. **Present only to their problems.** No feature dumps, no 50-line spec sheets. Case studies = same truck, same problem, real numbers, result.
7. **Check for agreement constantly** in longer messages/calls: "Does that make sense?" "You with me on that?" "Do you see how that'd fix the [their problem]?"

## 6. Referrals (how to ask and actually get them)

After a happy delivery:
1. "Really glad we got you dialed in. Can I ask — in your mind, how do you feel we helped you the most?" *(they tell THEMSELVES the value — now they own it)*
2. "With that in mind — who do you know that might be struggling with [the exact problem we solved]?"
3. "Tell me a bit about them and why you feel I could help?"
4. "How do you think it'd be best to approach them — do you feel you should reach out first and let them know I'll be in touch?" *(warm intro converts far better)*
5. "Can I suggest something? If you mention the challenges you were having and how we solved them, that'd probably be most helpful to them."
6. "Besides [name], is there anyone else YOU FEEL I could help?"

Contacting the referral — never assume interest:
> "Hey [name], this is Aaron at Tuned Yota — [referrer] suggested I reach out. I recently helped him with [problem] that was causing [effect], and he mentioned you might be running into the same thing with yours. Is this a decent time / is that something you're dealing with?"

## 7. Keeping Customers & Handling Complaints

**Relationship check-ins (retention):**
- "How do you feel the tune's been helping you the most? Why though?"
- "Anything you'd change or want us to look at to get even more out of it?"
- "What would you want the truck doing by next season that it isn't yet?"

**Upset customer = win the relationship, not the argument.** Their emotions are facts to them. Ask, don't defend:
- "What happened exactly, just so I know?" → "Can you say more about that so I understand?" → "What else can you tell me?"
- "I apologize this happened. What would you like to see done at this point so we can make it right for you?"
- "This bothers me too. How soon can we get it in so I can look at it personally?"
- Close the loop: "If anything else comes up in the meantime, will you come straight to me so I can handle it?"

## 8. Phrase Bank (use these constantly)

**Expanded probing starters (pull emotion out):** "Walk me through..." · "Tell me more..." · "Can you share with me..." · "Describe for me..." · "Give me an example..."

**Clarifiers (dig beneath vague words like 'trying', 'frustrating', 'challenging'):** "How do you mean exactly?" · "What's causing that?" · "Can I ask why?" · "Why is that important to you now though?" · "What would that do for you personally?"

**Softeners / status frames:** "just to see if I could even help you" · "might" · "possibly" · "would that be appropriate?" · "what you *might* be looking for"

**Hypothetical commitment openers (test before investing effort):** "Let's suppose..." · "What if..." · "Just imagine for a moment..." · "Assuming we were able to..." → always ended with: "...what do you see happening next?" / "where would we go from there?"

**Checking for agreement (sprinkle through any long explanation):** "Does that make sense?" · "Are we on the same page?" · "Do you see how that could help you?"

---

## 9. The Non-Negotiables (Do / Don't)

DO: ask before telling · end every message with one question · use their exact words · get a next-step commitment before sending anything of value · qualify urgency with "why now though?" · present only to stated problems · offer 3 quote options · stay neutral and unbothered.

DON'T: quote price cold · send info/links/references without a committed next step · pitch features · argue with objections · chase silent leads with "just following up!" (re-open their pain instead) · use pressure language · assume interest (even from referrals) · present more than ~15% of the interaction — questions should dominate.
